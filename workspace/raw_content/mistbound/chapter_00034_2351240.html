Maeryn didn’t quite know what to make of Samuel Parlor. He was tall and reasonably good-looking, neither muscular nor noticeably overweight. His beard and hair - both kept short, mostly dark blond, with strands of white shooting through it, suggesting he was approaching middle age - were kept tidy to the point of immaculate. He dressed well, but not ostentatiously so, and despite his noble status, he kept a sword at his hip.

If it weren’t for the sword, Maeryn would have been reasonably confident that he was a lower-class noble, or at least a noble who wasn’t so full of themselves that they needed to show it off. But something about the way he wore the sword, and moved with it… even though Samuel Parlor didn’t look terribly fit, he had experience with the blade. Maeryn would have bet on it.

“Captain Maeryn. I assume all is well? Are all preparations made for the trip tomorrow?” Parlor asked, his tone calm and professional. The two of them were in a meeting room at the recruitment center, as Maeryn had learned was the custom when it came to the contracts in Airspire, and the hunter just couldn’t seem to get a read on him.

“Yes, everything is fine. I’m simply here to finalize arrangements per our last discussion.” Maeryn tapped the paper in front of her. “We’ll be ready to leave first thing in the morning. I… do feel like I should remind you that the quarters we have are rather small, and that our airship isn’t designed for luxury or for, um, entertaining nobility,” she said hesitantly.

“It’s not a problem.” Parlor leaned forward, his expression unchanging. It was honestly somewhat intimidating. “As I’ve stated before, I’m more interested in getting to Zephyr than I am in being entertained along the way. Peace and quiet suits my purposes far more than watching hastily put together shows of drama and comedy.”

Maeryn breathed in deeply and nodded. “Alright then. What time do you want to leave tomorrow?”

“As early as possible. Will six work for you?”

“We can make that work,” Maeryn agreed.

“Then I believe our business is concluded. If there’s nothing else?”

“There’s nothing, no.” They both stood, and shook hands. “I’ll see you tomorrow morning, then.”

“Until then.” Parlor gave a brusque nod and strode out without turning back.

Maeryn watched him go with a frown. “I just can’t get a read on him… why is he so intense?” she muttered, before shaking her head. “Not my business. It’s not your business, Rin, don’t obsess over it. After the month is over you’ll probably never see him again anyway.”

And yet, despite her words, Maeryn knew she’d be keeping a close eye on Samuel Parlor. Maybe it was just paranoia, but she had a feeling that the nobleman was involved in something more than a simple business deal, and she wanted nothing to do with it.

On her way out of the recruitment center, Maeryn looked around for her captain acquaintance, Captain Jenkins of the airship Breegull, but the man was nowhere to be found. Maybe he’d gone to Cloudreach like she’d suggested, but Maeryn had kind of wanted to wish him farewell, especially since she was going to be gone for a few weeks.

Oh well. They’d meet again, Maeryn was certain of it.

As it was the last time she’d step foot on Airspire for most of a month, Maeryn took the rest of the afternoon to explore and see the sights. As she passed by the Zephyrian Research Center, she allowed a rueful smile to cross her face. It had been almost two weeks since poor Jake at reception had told her his woes about the broken coffee machine, and Maeryn had bought him coffee from every coffee shop nearby to see if it would help the man cope.

Jake had appreciated the gesture, but there was apparently something magical in the coffee machine - beyond being powered by runes - that gave him the extra kick he needed to be properly functional. The worst of his caffeine withdrawal had passed, but the unlucky receptionist was still perpetually exhausted.

Maeryn eventually made her way back to the Dancing Dragonflies inn, and she stood in front of it for a few minutes, contemplating. She hadn’t been on Airspire long, and yet… she’d gotten comfortable here. Unexpectedly so. Not quite as comfortable as home, but…

Maeryn stilled. She hadn’t really thought about her home in Vert for… How long had it been? Days, certainly. Maybe a week? Guilt bubbled in her stomach, and she grimaced, sitting down at a nearby bench.

Were her parents okay? Surely by now they’d read her letter to them. What did they think of her? Did they approve of what she was doing? Had they, and Frankie’s mother, and Dan’s colleagues in the Alchemical Workshop, had any or all of them tried to convince the Elders to send people to retrieve them?

What if there were other Geovans in Cloudreach right now, looking for them?

What if there weren’t? What if they’d written off Maeryn and her crew? Did they think Maeryn and her friends were dead?

Maeryn lowered her head, now wholeheartedly regretting that she hadn’t taken the time to speak to her parents before she left. Yes, maybe they could have somehow convinced her not to go on this crazy mission, but not knowing what they thought of her now… it hurt, in a way she couldn’t describe.

She took a deep, shuddering breath, wrestling back control of her emotions. She’d see them again. Someday. And then she’d be able to tell them about her adventure, and all the crazy things that had happened, and even if they thought that she’d been rash or immature, Maeryn would be able to show them that she’d made a difference, and then they’d forgive her. Right?

… Right?

Maeryn closed her eyes, trying not to acknowledge the wetness on her cheeks. She couldn’t go back. She’d made the best decisions she could. Her parents would understand that. They had to understand that. They’d raised her to be a problem-solver, and the Mist was a problem to be solved. She’d laid out all of her motivations and justifications in her letter to them.

And if they couldn’t… then where the abyss were they? A flicker of anger ignited in her heart, roiling around the grief that threatened to seize her lungs, and yet the two emotions harmonized instead of quenching each other, leaving Maeryn feeling like she was holding a storm inside her chest.

They were running out of time, and nobody had been doing what had to be done! Because now… now she couldn’t go home.

Maeryn’s fists, which had unconsciously clenched, loosened abruptly as the storm of emotion broke, leaving nothing but wholehearted grief, and she buried her face into her hands as she choked out a sob. It felt like she had to literally drag it out of her chest, and even though she knew it wasn’t actually tearing little gashes along her throat as she did, it still left her aching and sore inside.

She wanted to go home. She wanted her parents. But that wasn’t possible anymore.

Maeryn inhaled sharply, and wiped roughly at her cheeks. She couldn’t go back. She’d known that when she left. What was she doing? She was better than this. She was better than this. She couldn’t just sit around crying, when there were other things that she could be doing.

The more she was able to support her team, the faster they’d be able to solve the Mist problem, and the faster she’d be able to go back. She just had to focus on what she could do.

“Just focus, Rin…” she muttered fiercely to herself. “Just. Focus.”

With her homesickness abated, though not fully dispelled, Maeryn was able to get to her feet and walk into the Dancing Dragonflies inn. It was time to check on Dan and Ernesto’s progress.
