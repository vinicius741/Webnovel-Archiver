The sound of knocking roused Argent from her slumber. She sat up, momentarily disoriented by the unfamiliar surroundings. She took in the details one by one: the soft bed with its patchwork quilt, the worn wooden floor, the scent of lavender and woodsmoke. Memory returned in fragments, of the heist, the component, rain-soaked streets. This room at the inn, a temporary sanctuary she'd reluctantly accepted.

Outside her window, the steady drumming of rain continued unabated, her constant companion. She noticed puddles forming on the windowsill despite the shutters being closed tightly against the downpour. The golden light of late afternoon filtered through the gaps, casting long shadows across the floor. She'd slept far longer than intended.

"Room service," came Axel's cheerful voice from the other side of the door, muffled but unmistakable. "Dinner for the heroic angel, delivered by her equally heroic demonic colleague."

Argent smoothed her rumpled clothing and pushed an errant strand of hair behind her ear before opening the door, a futile gesture of normalcy. Axel stood balancing a wooden tray laden with food and a bottle of wine, his crimson hair even more unruly than usual. Water droplets clung to the fiery strands, sparkling like tiny embers in the afternoon light. His eyes creased at the corners as he smiled at her.

"I was beginning to think you'd sleep through to tomorrow," he said, breezing past her to set the tray on the small table by the window. His movements were fluid, graceful in a way that reminded her he wasn't human, despite his casual demeanor. "The bath is ready when you are, but I thought you might want sustenance first."

The aroma of roasted meat, herbs, and fresh bread made Argent's stomach growl embarrassingly. She couldn't remember the last time she'd eaten properly. "Thank you," she said quietly, eyeing the spread as Axel poured wine into two earthenware cups. Steam rose from the food and her mouth watered.

"The innkeeper's finest," he proclaimed with mock solemnity, offering her a cup. The glazed pottery was warm against her fingers. "Which isn't saying much, but beggars can't be choosers."

Argent accepted the wine cautiously, taking a small sip. It was sweeter than she expected, with an undertone of berries and something earthy she couldn't name. The warmth spread through her chest, battling the chill that had settled into her bones. "It's good," she said, surprised.

"Damning with faint praise," Axel laughed. He raised his own cup in a toast. "To successful heists and unlikely alliances."

After a moment's hesitation Argent tapped her cup against his. The ceramic made a soft clink in response. "To returning what was stolen," she offered, her voice softer than intended.

They ate in a silence that gradually shifted from awkward to comfortable. Argent was surprised by her own appetite as she tore into the roast chicken, soaking up the juices with the crusty bread. As she ate, she found herself sneaking glances at Axel, who seemed entirely at ease, one leg slung casually over the arm of his chair. His long fingers tore pieces of bread with deliberate grace, and she noticed that he seemed to be merely eating to keep her company; he left most of the food to her.

"Why are you really here?" she asked finally, setting down her cup. The question had been circling her mind since their first encounter in the tavern. "The truth this time."

Axel's ever-present smile dimmed slightly, revealing something more complex. He studied the wine in his cup as if it might contain the answer, swirling it gently as he stared into its depths. "Would you believe me if I said I was curious?"

"About what?" Argent pressed, her eyes fixed on his face, searching for deception.

"About you." He looked up, his red eyes meeting hers directly. There was no challenge in them, no mockery, just genuine interest that made her inexplicably uneasy. "Angels don't usually walk alone among mortals. They certainly don't concern themselves with stolen machine parts or struggling towns."

The observation struck closer to home than she cared to admit. Her shoulders tensed slightly, causing a faint trickle of rainwater to seep through the ceiling above her, a physical manifestation of her discomfort. "You were watching me before we met at the tavern."

It wasn't a question, but Axel nodded anyway, unashamed. "You stood out. An angel trying very hard to be inconspicuous, yet helping humans without any apparent agenda." He tilted his head curiously, reminding her of a bird examining something shiny. "Most of your kind look down on mortals. See them as pets, or at best, helpless children who need guidance."

"And demons don't?" she countered, a defensive edge creeping into her voice.

"Oh, we're definitely worse," he admitted with a wry smile that showed teeth just slightly too sharp to be human. "We see them as playthings or food. But you..." He gestured with his cup, the movement encircling her. "You care about them. Genuinely. It's fascinating."

Argent wasn't sure how to respond. The attention made her feel strangely exposed, as if he could see the doubts and questions that had haunted her since her crossing over. "They deserve better than they get," she said at last, tracing a pattern in the condensation on the table. "From both angels and demons."

Axel watched her for a long moment, his expression unreadable. The silence stretched between them. Then he smiled again. "Well, they got better today, thanks to us. The mill will be running again, no more bandits harassing travelers, happy endings all around." He raised his cup again. "We make a good team, angel-face."

The nickname should have irritated her, but instead it brought an unexpected warmth to her cheeks. Before she could reply he rose from his seat with fluid grace. "Your bath will be getting cold," he said, nodding toward the door. "I had them add lavender oil." He winked, the gesture somehow both playful and intimate. "Good for relaxing tense angels."

With that, he was gone, the door closing softly behind him, leaving Argent alone with her thoughts once more - thoughts that had become unexpectedly complicated in his presence.

The bath was indeed wonderful. The lavender oil filled the small bathing chamber with its soothing scent, mingling with the steam that rose from the wooden tub. As she sank into the water, muscles she hadn't realized were tense began to unwind. She found herself wondering what it would be like to simply exist without purpose or mission, to experience the world not as an angel but just as herself. The thought was both liberating and terrifying.

By the time Argent emerged night had fallen completely. The rain had eased to a mist but a strange restlessness had taken hold of her, making the small room feel suddenly confining. There was a soft tap at the door. When she opened it, she found Axel leaning against the doorframe, a brown bottle dangling from his fingers. "Fancy a nightcap under the stars?" he asked, gesturing toward the inn's roof with the bottle.

Argent raised an eyebrow, momentarily forgetting her inner turmoil. "On the roof?"

"Best view in town," he confirmed with a grin. "I checked while you were taking the world's longest bath."

She should say no. It was inappropriate, unnecessary, potentially dangerous. Her rational mind listed all the reasons to decline: he was a demon, she was an angel, they were natural enemies, she barely knew him, she couldn't trust his motives. But the thought of sitting alone in her room with only her doubts for company held no appeal, and something in Axel's expression made her hesitate. "Just for a little while," she agreed finally, the words surprising her even as she spoke them.

"That's the spirit, angel-face." He gestured gallantly. “After you.”

They climbed out through a window at the end of the hall, Axel going first to offer her a hand she didn't need but accepted anyway. The contact sent an unexpected shiver through her. His skin was warm, nearly hot to the touch in contrast to her chilled fingers.

Axel seemed to sense her thoughts. He passed her the bottle, their fingers brushing momentarily. "It's not your fault, you know," he said quietly. "The storm. It's just part of who you are."

Argent took a long sip from the bottle, letting the burn of the alcohol distract her from the weight of his words. "That doesn't make it any less destructive," she replied, passing the bottle back.

"No," he agreed, surprising her with his honesty. "But it doesn't make you a monster either."

The simple statement struck something deep within her. How long had it been since someone had spoken to her as a person, not as an angel or a weapon or a problem? The Council had always viewed her storm-bringing nature as a liability; a power to be controlled and directed, not understood or accepted.

Feathers torn, bones broken methodically, one by one, while dispassionate voices recorded her responses.
