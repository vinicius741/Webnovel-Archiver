Southeastern Hatching Chamber, Shashan, Shashvat Ananda

The botanical dress clung to Xin's frame like armor, its violet fibers moving with him. He adjusted his glasses, glad the Weavers had let him keep his things - the green Quantum Watch on his wrist, the silver-green Psi Shield Device behind his ear, and Pawan, his Omni-drone hovering beside him.

"Okay. Let's see what's going on around here," Xin muttered, approaching the massive structure housing the Hatching Chamber.

Glowing spores drifted through the purple air. The architecture was unlike anything on Earth - no steel or concrete, just living tissue that pulsed with an inner light. Twisted pillars rose from the ground like trees, their surfaces glistening with a membrane that reflected the light.

Organic veins carried processed Helionite like lifeblood. The green glow filtered through layers of living tissue until it looked almost beautiful - streams of energy feeding the chamber while powering its functions. The structure seemed to breathe with its own fusion reaction.

Xin's Quantum Watch flickered, its sensors struggling to make sense of this blend of organic and crystalline power. This wasn't just a new approach to energy - it was a completely different relationship with it.

A figure emerged from between the pillars.

She was both attractive and alien - tall and curvaceous, with wet alabaster skin as pale as moonlight. Her eyes were dark pools that seemed to absorb light, though occasional blue irises peeked through. Where human nipples would be, eye-like orbs rested on her breasts, their pupils sometimes dilating as if watching him. Below her waist, her form became serpentine, a long body that moved smoothly across the violet grass. Soft tentacles swayed on her back.

"So," her British accent carried warmth and authority, "you're the tinker who claims he can fix my chamber." Her black hair flowed down her shoulders like liquid darkness, moving slightly without wind. When she smiled, her forked tongue flickered between her lips.

Xin fought the urge to step back. "I'm — Zhi-Xin Wu," he said, his voice steadier than he felt. "Though most just call me Xin."

"Kathrin. Elder Sūkṛmuc in service to Primarch Moro," she replied, sliding closer. "Tell me, Earth-Dweller, what makes you think you can understand our technology?" Her tongue darted out again, and Xin couldn't help but watch it.

Pawan beeped softly beside him, probably sensing his elevated heart rate. The drone's presence was comforting amid all this strangeness.

"I understand patterns. Engineering patterns," Xin said, trying to maintain eye contact rather than looking at her unsettling features. "Systems have logic, whether they're silicon or..." he gestured at the living architecture, "organic."

Kathrin laughed melodiously. "Spoken like an engineer." She moved around him, her serpentine lower body making patterns in the grass. "But our chambers aren't mere machines. They're extensions of life itself." She paused, studying him. "The eggs within are mine, and those of my consorts."

"Consorts? As in... plural?" Xin couldn't hide his surprise.

Kathrin smiled, amused. "Each contributing their unique strengths to our progeny." She gestured toward the chamber. "What Earth humans call monogamy is rather limiting, don't you think?"

That revelation sent a shiver down Xin's spine. This wasn't just about fixing a mechanical problem - this was about someone's personal reproductive system.

"Show me, then, Lady Kathrin," he said quietly. "Let me help."

"Lady? How quaint." Kathrin's expression changed, something unreadable crossing her features. She extended a pale hand toward him, fingers long and elegant despite their alien nature. "Come then, young Xin. Let's see if your patterns can dance with ours."

They approached what looked like the chamber's entrance - three oval archways of living tissue that pulsed with violet light.

As they passed through the central archway, Kathrin eyed Pawan. "Remain outside, little drone. The Chamber rejects artificial life forms like you."

Xin reluctantly agreed. "Pawan. Hold position."

The drone obeyed with what sounded like a sad hum, hovering in place.

The membrane at the entrance parted like silk as Xin passed through. Inside, the chamber rose upward in organic spires, like ancient temples with curves and towers. Violet light pulsed through translucent veins in the walls, while openings in the ceiling let in filtered light from the purple sky.

At the chamber's center, a pedestal of crystalline material held what appeared to be a fragment of something larger - a shard of crystal that pulsed with soft blue light against the chamber's violet hues.

"Is that...?" Xin's question trailed off as he approached the pedestal.

"A fragment of the Moondust Crystal. Yes." Kathrin moved beside him, her serpentine form coiling slightly. "A scholar named Harald Omdal came and left us with it. Vyom and I take turns watching the shard to monitor your Sol System."

Xin looked closer, fascinated by the shard's inner glow. "This is what everyone's fighting over back home."

"Were I able, I'd spend more time with it," Kathrin said, guiding him toward a central platform where several clusters of large, iridescent eggs rested in cradle-like depressions. "Though lately, something's gone wrong with the poor dears."

Xin approached one of the cradles, noticing how his Quantum Watch's display flickered strangely. The eggs were about the size of basketballs, with shimmering purple shells that changed colors. They were unusually still.

"These clusters seem different from each other," he observed, noting variations in size, color, and arrangement.

"Different fathers," Kathrin replied matter-of-factly. "Seventeen different males have contributed to my current egg clutches recently."

Xin nearly stumbled. "Seventeen? At once?"

Kathrin laughed, the sound echoing through the chamber. "Is that so shocking, dear?" She gestured to the different clusters. "Those beautiful violet ones are from Najan, my first bond-mate this decade. The larger ones with blue markings are Eklavya's—you've met him, I believe. The crimson-speckled clutch is from my union with Vyomendri."

She moved between the clusters with obvious pride. "And here we have contributions from Shekhar, Aditya, Farrokh, Jagannath and several others—all magnificent specimens."

"And...they're all okay with this arrangement?" Xin asked, unable to hide his surprise.

"Darling, they're honored," Kathrin's eyes gleamed with amusement. "In Rakshasa society, females select multiple worthy males to ensure genetic diversity. The Bahuvivāhaḥ, as we call it—keeps our bloodlines strong and our social bonds intact." She ran her hand over one of the eggs lovingly. "Each male contributes unique strengths to our collective future."

Xin's mind reeled. "And everyone just accepts this?"

"Jealousy is a concept rooted in scarcity, dear boy," Kathrin's tone was almost pitying. "When existence spans centuries rather than decades, one learns that love and passion needn't be limited."

Several Weavers - bee-like creatures with violet bodies - hovered near the eggs, their wings creating a soft humming that suddenly stuttered.

Xin noticed how the organic floor responded to his steps, adjusting to support his weight. "I can respect that."

Kathrin replied, curling around one of the egg cradles. "And now we're past pleasantries."

Xin approached one of the cradles, noticing how his Quantum Watch's green display flickered oddly. "May I?" he asked, reaching toward one.

"Careful," Kathrin cautioned. "They're sensitive to temporal fluctuations."

When his fingers touched the shell, his Quantum Watch scrambled completely. The egg felt too cool. "The time signatures here are strange," he observed. "How do you maintain the chamber's internal rhythms?"

"The Weavers," Kathrin replied, pointing to the violet bee-like creatures. "They maintain a precise harmonic frequency. Essential for proper development."

Xin watched the Weavers' flight patterns with growing interest. Their movements followed specific paths like mandala patterns, but there was something off about their rhythm.

Suddenly, the Moondust shard on the pedestal flared with intense blue light. Voices - distant yet somehow present - came from it:

"...prefer my brain where it is," a male voice said, one Xin didn't recognize.

Then, unmistakably, Ume's voice responded: "Such fascinating organism! — perhaps we could take a sample back to study it?"

Xin rushed to the pedestal. "That's Ume! Where is she? Why is this shard playing…?"

Kathrin glided to his side. "When activated, any fragment allows us glimpses across vast distances, seeing through the eyes of those around it." She peered at the crystal. "A Maridian male carried another shard. He calls himself Jabari."

"Jabari?" Xin remembered the name from his last encounter with Ume. "She found him after we separated. Or rather, they found her."

Xin approached cautiously, his watch suddenly making a high-pitched whine. "Ah, the Helionite's been contaminated with an isotope. Probably something from your local environment that's similar enough to fool your organic sensors."
